## Email 3: Inventory Cash Trap

### Subject Line Options

- Inventory Is Cash Wearing a Cardboard Box Costume
- Before You Reorder Inventory, Check This
- Restocking Can Break Your Cash Flow
- The Inventory Order Might Be the Problem

### Body

Hey {{first_name}},

Inventory is weird.

You need it to protect revenue.

But buying it can wreck your cash position.

That is the ecommerce operator’s favorite little knife fight.

If you restock too late, you risk stockouts.

If you restock too early, you trap cash in inventory before the business actually needs it.

And if you place the full purchase order without checking the next 13 weeks of cash flow, you may accidentally starve the business of money for ads, payroll, shipping, refunds, payment obligations, or taxes.

Before placing your next inventory order, check:

1. Current units
2. Average weekly units sold
3. Weeks of cover
4. Supplier lead time
5. Reorder point
6. Purchase order amount
7. Payment date
8. Projected cash after purchase
9. Minimum safe cash buffer

The planner helps you see whether the order is safe, risky, or needs to be split, delayed, reduced, or negotiated.

Do not ask, “Can I afford the order today?”

Ask:

“Can I afford the order and still survive the next several weeks before this inventory turns back into cash?”

That is the better question.

Jason

## Email 4: Ad Spend Cash Trap

### Subject Line Options

- ROAS Is Not Cash
- Your Ads Might Be Profitable and Still Create a Cash Crunch
- Before You Scale Ads, Check Cash Timing
- Paid Ads Spend Now. Payouts Arrive Later.

### Body

Hey {{first_name}},

ROAS is not cash.

Read that again before Meta sweet-talks you into scaling a campaign your bank account cannot survive.

Ads can be profitable on paper and still create cash pressure.

Why?

Because ad spend leaves now.

Revenue may settle later.

Profit arrives after COGS, platform fees, refunds, shipping, and payout delays.

And if you are scaling ads while inventory is tight, you may be paying to create demand you cannot fulfill.

Before increasing ad spend, check:

- Weekly ad spend
- Ad spend as a percentage of net revenue
- Gross margin
- Platform payout timing
- Inventory availability
- Cash runway
- Refund rate
- Payment obligations during the same week

If cash is tight, do not automatically kill all ads.

Cut weak campaigns first.

Keep proven campaigns if inventory, margin, and cash timing support them.

The planner helps you separate “ads are bad” from “cash timing is bad.”

Different problems. Different fixes.

Jason

## Email 5: Payment Stacking

### Subject Line Options

- Recurring Payments Quietly Eat Operating Cash
- Is Financing Helping or Choking Your Cash Flow?
- Payment Stacking Is the Silent Killer
- Before You Take More Capital, Stress-Test the Payment

### Body

Hey {{first_name}},

Payments do not just cost money.

They cost flexibility.

A weekly payment here, a platform repayment there, a credit card minimum, a line of credit draw, a merchant cash advance, a PayPal or Shopify repayment...

Individually, each payment may look manageable.

Together, they can quietly choke your operating cash.

That is payment stacking.

Before taking on more obligations, you need to know:

1. Current weekly payments
2. Payment frequency
3. Remaining balances
4. Whether payments are fixed or sales-based
5. What new payments would do to weekly cash
6. Whether payments collide with inventory purchases
7. Whether repayment would push cash below your safe buffer

Capital can help a timing gap.

But if repayment creates a bigger squeeze, you are just moving the problem into a darker alley and giving it a stick.

Use the Payment Obligations tab.

Then stress-test your payments before saying yes to more capital.

## Email 6: Working Capital Gap

### Subject Line Options

- Do You Have a Working Capital Gap?
- Your Cash Gap Is Not Always a Revenue Problem
- How Much Cash Do You Actually Need?
- Find the Week Your Cash Gets Tight

### Body

Hey {{first_name}},

A working capital gap is not the same as “I need money.”

It means the forecast shows a timing gap between cash available and cash required.

For ecommerce sellers, that gap often comes from:

- Inventory paid before sales convert back to cash
- Ads charged before revenue settles
- Platform payouts delayed
- Recurring payments hitting every week
- Refunds and fees reducing available cash
- Supplier payments landing before customer cash does

The planner estimates a gap like this:

Minimum safe cash buffer  
minus  
lowest projected cash balance  
equals  
planning gap before cushion

Then you can add a planning cushion if data is messy or timing risk is high.

Example:

Minimum safe cash buffer: $15,000  
Lowest projected cash: $4,000  
Planning gap: $11,000  
Cushion: 20%  
Suggested planning range: about $11,000-$13,200

That is not an approval.

That is not a guarantee.

That is not a lender decision.

It is a planning number.

And planning numbers beat panic numbers every day of the week.

Jason
